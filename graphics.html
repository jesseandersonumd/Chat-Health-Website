  • First and foremost, look at the title. What is it telling you? To figure out what the title actually means in context, try to find annotations - these are captions that are generally below graphs or in categories like ‘data definitions’ on a state’s website. This can help you figure out what the shape of the graph might mean.
  • Now the shape is really important, but it can be tricky to figure out unless you’ve given yourself some much needed context. If you’re looking at a graph and you see a flat line, look closely - is it talking about new cases? If so, the situation actually isn’t improving. Think about it like this: if we had 125 new cases of COVID-19 one day and then 127 the next, the graph would still look like a flat-line, but our situation would not be improving.
  • If things are looking up, then our actual line would be decreasing, not just flatlining. This is something you’d be able to figure out after reading what the axes are (how does x relate to y?), what the title says, and what the annotation tells us.
